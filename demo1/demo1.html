An Overview

Falconry is the use of trained raptors for game hunting. Traditionally, the term "falcon" referred to a female bird, which were favored for hunting (due to their larger size), while the term tiercel described a male bird. The term "falcon" now applies to any bird (male or female) belonging to a species of raptor in the genus Falco.

The sport has a long history and was regarded as a status symbol among the nobles of medieval Europe, the Middle East, and Mongolian Empire due to the necessary commitments of time, money and space. According to Wikipedia, Frederick II of Hohenstaufen (1194â€“1250) is generally acknowledged as the most significant wellspring of traditional falconry knowledge. He is believed to have obtained firsthand knowledge of Arabic falconry during wars in the region. He commissioned and edited the translation of Moamyn's manual on falconry, known in Latin as De Scientia Venandi per Aves, and eventually wrote his own treatise on falconry, De arte venandi cum avibus.

The rise of firearms led to the decline of the popularity of falconry, but it is still practiced around the world. Advances in technology and vetrinary practices have allowed modern falconers to pursue quarry and styles of flight that would previously have resulted in the loss of their birds.

Choice Birds

Despite the terminology, falconry is not limited to just falcons. Throughout history, many different types of raptors have been kept and trained for hunting. Over time, falconers have come to class them in four three general categories.

  • Broadwings
    • Golden Eagles
    • Buzzards
    • Harris hawks
  • Longwings
  • Shortwings

For more information on the different categories, as well as some less commonly used birds, visit this article about birds used in contemporary falconry on Wikipedia.org.
